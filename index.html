Introduction



1


Intelligent systems are computer based systems that are highly connected, have massive processing power and perform complex applications. They have the ability to represent, reason about and interpret data. They analyze the data and try to derive its meaning so that they can come up with strategies to act on the results of the analysis.

Intelligent systems have many applications, from processing large databases to controlling robots and drones. The ideas and concepts are drawn from the areas of artificial intelligence, machine learning, and a range of fields such as psychology, linguistics and brain sciences, forming many interdisciplinary relationships. They also play a large role in our day to day lives, especially in areas such as transportation, health and entertainment.2

History1

How it all began...

  • 1955 - 1956

    The Birth of Artificial Intelligence

    John McCarthy coined the term "Artificial Intelligence" in his 1955 proposal for the 1956 Dartmouth Artificial Intelligence conference, which gave birth to the field.

  • 1956 â€“ 1974

    The Golden Age

    During this period, people were amazed that computers were able to accomplish impressive tasks like solving problems and proving theorems in geometry and algebra.
    This period also marked the completion of the world's first full-scale anthropomorphic robot built in the world, WABOT-1.

  • 1974 - 1980

    The First AI Winter

    The field had little progress due to limited computing power and the lack of data.
    The constraint in computing power led to tremendous amount of time required to find optimal solutions to many problems. This also meant that many AI solutions could never be scaled up into useful systems.
    The lack of data was an impeding factor to the progress of vision and natural language AI applications, which required a vast amount of information.

  • 1980 - 1987

    The AI Boom

    Expert system, a form of AI program that attempts to act like a human expert on a particular subject area2, began to be widely adopted by organisations around the world.
    The popularisation of a form of neural network (Hopfield network) and a new method for training them (backpropagation) led to the progress in optical character and speech recognition.

  • 1987 - 1993

    The Second AI Winter

    With the rise of the microcomputers, desktop computers became more powerful than the more expensive Lisp machines that used specialised AI hardware. This led to the sudden collapse of a half a billion dollars industry, and the cutting of research funds.

  • 1993 - 2000

    The Rise of AI

    Algorithms developed by AI researchers started to integrate into larger systems and industrial processes such as data mining, medical diagnosis and Google's search engine.

  • 2000 â€“ Present

    Big Data & Deep Learning

    Access to large amount of data and advanced machine learning techniques, paired with greater computing power, led to the successful progress and research of various applications and problems.

COMPUTER VISION

Computer vision is an advanced technology that enables computers to gain information from images, videos or multi-dimensional data in any other form and subsequently solve problem with that useful information.1


Pedestrian detection

Detecting and tracking people is an important area of research and has been a key problem in Computer Vision2. According to the department of transport in the UK, there are more than 25,000 people killed or seriously injured in the year ending June 2017.3 Hence, there is a considerable interest in building automated vision systems for pedestrian detection.

ROBOTICS

Movies like Star Wars and Terminator come to mind when we think about robots. What most people consider robots are mostly science fiction as many of these examples only capture our imagination. Although there is no standard definition for what we call a robot, it can be considered as an artificial agent that can sense, move, power itself and is intelligent. We still do not have the capabilities to give common sense to a robot so that it can interact with the dynamic world like we do. Nevertheless, robots will soon be everywhere in our lives and no doubt they will tremendously change the way we live.6

There is a high anticipation for the near future with the latest advanced AI technologies being translated to robotics.7 Most of the warehouses will be automated and the robots will do all the mechanic and repetitive jobs, we will have self-driving vehicles and achieve autonomous public transport in the cities, and robots will be able to assist humans in ways such as performing rescue missions and autonomous surgical operations.

Natural Language Processing

Natural Language Processing (NLP) is a form of artificial intelligence and a field that involves interactions between computers and human language. It enables computer to analyse and understand natural language like English or Chinese as well as deriving meaning from it in an intelligent way. By utilising NLP, speech recognition, translation and auto-summarisation can be accomplished. However, human languages are rarely precise. The same sentence spoken in different tones can make a huge difference in meaning. As a result, it remains a challenge for machines to fully understand conversations, and optimising NLP is still an ongoing process.10

Health1

The involvement of Intelligent Systems in the Health industry can be represented by three main categories:

  • Early and Precise Diagnosis
  • Dynamic Care
  • Artificial Intelligence Health Assistants

Early and precise diagnosis

Artificial Intelligence algorithms are used to analyse medical data and among millions of samples, find useful patterns. This can result in earlier detection and more accurate diagnosis, which helps to offer the best opportunity for successful treatments. Such systems, unlike humans, do not lose their accuracy but get wiser over time.

A lot of research has been carried out across the globe. A few examples include a skin cancer detection mobile application, currently being developed by Stanford University, and a DeepMind project, in collaboration with UKâ€™s National Health Service (NHS), to fight blindness using machine learning which might eventually be able to prevent 98% of most simple visual loss.


Dynamic care

IBM's Watson for Oncology is a platform that aids in treating cancer by presenting effective treatment options after analysis. The platform was tested in the University of North Carolina School of Medicine and gave the same recommendations as professional oncologists in 99% of the cases. This is critical to health centres that do not have enough resources.




Artificial Intelligent health assistants

Automated machines are now being used in hospitals for basic check-ups. There are cases in which small clinics are unable to manage the amount of patients and these machines help to save time for patients as they no longer have to wait to be examined by a doctor for basic illnesses. This also helps to free up doctors so that they can be better used in areas such as performing emergency surgeries.

Your.MD chatbot is one example of an AI health assistant. The medical conditions extracted from the patients are automatically analysed and relevant information is then provided to them. If the assistant decides that the user needs to visit a doctor, it will automatically warn them. The diagnosis of Your.MD has been approved by the NHS to be authentic and reliable.

E-Commerce4

The introduction of AI has changed the way e-commerce works as processes previously done by salesman are now carried out by intelligent systems. Marketing companies use such systems to provide a more targeted way to advertise by personalising customer experience through the recommendation of products and offers based on the customers' past behaviour. Targeted advertisements, in the form of products that you have recently searched for in online shops, can be frequently seen on social media sites. This approach benefits both the customers as well as the companies trying to promote their products.

Mintigo is a company that provides predictive marketing services to popular firms to increase their sales. They use these systems to monitor and track down potential clients based on their interests and preferences. In this way, they can ensure that the target audience of their advertisements will be interested in the products featured, leading to higher sales rate.

Entertainment6


Subscription video on demand service providers, like Netflix, Amazon Prime Video and Hulu, have taken entertainment to a whole new level. In the past, people had to stay at home in order to watch their favourite television series and are frequently interrupted by annoying advertisements. Not only did the service providers remove the need to be confined at home, they have also maximised the user's experience by using complex systems and data analysis to suggest movies or series that the user might be interested in.

Although this works out pretty well in most cases, there can be some anomalies in the human behaviour and mood that cannot be predicted by machines. This can be changed with the next-generation algorithms that are being developed these days. These evolved algorithms break down a movie or television series into smaller pieces and try to map them to the user's preferences much more effectively. This allows us to predict that in the near future, these systems will be able to take the userâ€™s mood, circumstances and location into consideration to provide more accurate suggestions.

Transportation8


A self-driving car, also known as an autonomous or driverless car, is a vehicle that uses sensors to navigate without the need for human intervention. Autonomous vehicles such as the automated trucks serve as a wake-up call for the transport industry. Automated trucks save people from having to drive exhausting distances and also make travelling safer as they will not be affected by fatigue.

Using sophisticated algorithms to calculate the fastest routes, while also taking into consideration the traffic and all means of transport, is now a feature that exists in almost all navigation applications. One such example is Google Maps, which has become much more "intelligent" than it used to be with the integration of AI in its software. Google Maps allows you to plan your trip ahead of time by providing real-time as well as predicted data.

John McCarthy

John McCarthy was generally recognised as the Father of Artificial Intelligence.2 He co-authored the paper A Proposal for the Dartmouth Summer Research Project on Artificial Intelligence in 1955 and is commonly cited as the one who organised the 1956 Dartmouth Summer Research Project.3 Those eventually led to the birth of the field devoted to the development of intelligent machines.

For his contributions to the Artificial Intelligence field, he was awarded the 1971 Turing Award4, the highest distinction of awards for computer science and widely recognised as the Nobel Prize of computing.

Geoffrey Hinton

Geoffrey Hinton is currently a Lead Scientist in the Google Brain AI Team as well as a Professor at the University of Toronto.6 | 7 He is known as the Godfather of Deep Learning8 for his breakthrough publications that led to the great advancement in the field. His publications have widely influenced the way many applications operate, such as the recommendation systems and image recognition.9

Key publications:
Learning representations by back-propagating errors
A fast learning algorithm for deep belief nets

Judea Pearl

Judea Pearl is a Professor and the current Director of the Cognitive Systems Laboratory at UCLA.11 He is best known for being the pioneer in the probabilistic approach to Artificial Intelligence and the development of Bayesian networks, a model to represent relationships.12 An example would be the use of it to compute the relationships between diseases and symptoms.

For his contributions to the Artificial Intelligence field, he was awarded the 2011 Turing Award.13

Key publication:
Probabilistic Reasoning in Intelligent Systems: Networks of Plausible Inference

Andrew Ng

Andrew Ng is the current Co-Chairman and Founder of Coursera, an extremely popular website that offers online education, and an Adjunct Professor at Stanford University.15 He was the former Chief Scientist of Baidu, the Google of China, where he led the company's Artificial Intelligence Group.16 He also founded and led the Google Brain project, which resulted in the capability of detecting cats after learning from unlabelled YouTube videos.17

For his contributions to the Artificial Intelligence field, he was awarded the 2009 IJCAI Computers and Thought Award18, which is among the highest recognitions any AI researcher may receive.

Key publication:
Latent Dirichlet Allocation

Yann LeCun

Yann LeCun is the first and current Director of AI Research at Facebook as well as a Professor at New York University.20 He was in Geoffrey Hinton's lab as a postdoctoral research associate and is known for his work on computer vision.21

Key publication:
Gradient-Based Learning Applied to Document Recognition

Stuart Russell

Stuart Russell is a Professor at the University of California, Berkeley and the author of Artificial Intelligence: A Modern Approach, a leading textbook in Artificial Intelligence used in over 1300 universities.23

For his contributions to the Artificial Intelligence field, he was awarded the 1995 IJCAI Computers and Thought Award.24
