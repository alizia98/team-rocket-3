Introduction




An intelligent system is a technologically advanced computer-based system that can represent, reason about, and interpret data. In doing so it can learn about the structure of the data, analyse the data to extract patterns and meaning, derive new information, and identify strategies and behaviours to act on the results of its analysis.

Intelligent systems come in many forms and have many applications, from processing huge data sets to controlling robots and drones. The ideas and concepts are drawn from the areas of artificial intelligence, machine learning, and a range of fields such as psychology, linguistics and brain sciences, forming many interdisciplinary relationships.1

History

How it all began...

  • 1955 - 1956

    The Birth of Artificial Intelligence

    John McCarthy coined the term "Artificial Intelligence" in his 1955 proposal for the 1956 Dartmouth Artificial Intelligence conference, which gave birth to the field.

  • 1956 â€“ 1974

    The Golden Age

    During this period, people were amazed that computers were able to accomplish impressive tasks like solving problems and proving theorems in geometry and algebra.
    This period also marked the completion of the world's first full-scale anthropomorphic robot built in the world, WABOT-1.

  • 1974 - 1980

    The First AI Winter

    The field had little progress due to limited computing power and the lack of data.
    The constraint in computing power led to tremendous amount of time required to find optimal solutions to many problems. This also meant that many AI solutions could never be scaled up into useful systems.
    The lack of data was an impeding factor to the progress of vision and natural language AI applications, which required a vast amount of information.

  • 1980 - 1987

    The AI Boom

    Expert system, a form of AI program that attempts to act like a human expert on a particular subject area, began to be widely adopted by organisations around the world.
    The popularisation of a form of neural network (Hopfield network) and a new method for training them (backpropagation) led to the progress in optical character and speech recognition.

  • 1987 - 1993

    The Second AI Winter

    With the rise of the microcomputers, desktop computers became more powerful than the more expensive Lisp machines that used specialised AI hardware. This led to the sudden collapse of a half a billion dollars industry, and the cutting of research funds.

  • 1993 - 2000

    The Rise of AI

    Algorithms developed by AI researchers started to integrate into larger systems and industrial processes such as data mining, medical diagnosis and Google's search engine.

  • 2000 â€“ Present

    Big Data & Deep Learning

    Access to large amount of data and advanced machine learning techniques, paired with greater computing power, led to the successful progress and research of various applications and problems.

  • Early and Precise Diagnosis
  • Dynamic Care
  • Artificial Intelligence Health Assistants

Early and precise diagnosis

Artificial Intelligence algorithms are used in order to analyse medical data and among millions of samples find useful patterns. This can result in earlier and more accurate diagnosis. Having an early diagnosis can result in complete cure, while late or wrong diagnosis can have damaging or even fatal results for the patient. Such machines unlike humans donâ€™t lose their accuracy when they grow old but get wiser over time.

A lot of research is carried out in universities in this section and an example of a successful application of AI is that of Stanford University which is really close in realising a mobile app that can be used in order to detect skin cancer. Another example is DeepMind who in cooperation with NHS are working on a project to fight blindness using machine learning. According to experts they might manage to develop the deep learning algorithm and be able to prevent 98% of most simple visual loss.

In addition, the AI platform created named Morpheo helps in the diagnosis of irregularities when sleeping. This automated platform is used to assist doctors by identifying the sleep patterns of patients avoiding the traditional process which took much more hours and complexity to perform. The people behind this machine learning tool expect it to help in creating predictive and preventive treatments.

John McCarthy

John McCarthy is generally recognised as the Father of Artificial Intelligence. He co-authored the paper A Proposal for the Dartmouth Summer Research Project on Artificial Intelligence in 1955 and is commonly cited as the one who organised the 1956 Dartmouth Summer Research Project. Those eventually led to the birth of the field devoted to the development of intelligent machines.

For his contributions to the Artificial Intelligence field, he was awarded the 1971 Turing Award, the highest distinction of awards for computer science and widely recognised as the Nobel Prize of computing.

Geoffrey Hinton

Geoffrey Hinton is currently a Lead Scientist in the Google Brain AI Team as well as a Professor at the University of Toronto. He is known as the Godfather of Deep Learning for his breakthrough publications that led to the great advancement in the field. His publications have widely influenced the way many applications operate, such as the recommendation systems and image recognition.

Key publications:
Learning representations by back-propagating errors
A fast learning algorithm for deep belief nets

Yann LeCun

Yann LeCun is the first and current Director of AI Research at Facebook as well as a Professor at New York University. He was in Geoffrey Hinton's lab as a postdoctoral research associate and is known for his work on computer vision.

Key publication:
Gradient-Based Learning Applied to Document Recognition

Andrew Ng

Andrew Ng is the current Co-Chairman and Founder of Coursera, an extremely popular website that offers online education, and an Adjunct Professor at Stanford University. He was the former Chief Scientist of Baidu, the Google of China, where he led the company's Artificial Intelligence Group. He also founded and led the Google Brain project, which resulted in the capability of detecting cats after learning from unlabelled YouTube videos.

For his contributions to the Artificial Intelligence field, he was awarded the 2009 IJCAI Computers and Thought Award, which is among the highest recognitions any AI researcher may receive.

Key publication:
Latent Dirichlet Allocation

Judea Pearl

Judea Pearl is a Professor and the current Director of the Cognitive Systems Laboratory at UCLA. He is best known for being the pioneer in the probabilistic approach to Artificial Intelligence and the development of Bayesian networks, a model to represent relationships. An example would be the use of it to compute the relationships between diseases and symptoms.

For his contributions to the Artificial Intelligence field, he was awarded the 2011 Turing Award.

Key publication:
Probabilistic Reasoning in Intelligent Systems: Networks of Plausible Inference

Stuart Russell

Stuart Russell is a Professor at the University of California, Berkeley and the author of Artificial Intelligence: A Modern Approach, a leading textbook in Artificial Intelligence used in over 1300 universities.

For his contributions to the Artificial Intelligence field, he was awarded the 1995 IJCAI Computers and Thought Award.
